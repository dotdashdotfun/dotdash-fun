What is Morse Code?

Morse code is a method of encoding text using sequences of dots and dashes — short and long signals that represent letters, numbers, and punctuation. Simple in concept, yet powerful enough to cross oceans and save lives.

The Inventor

Samuel Finley Breese Morse was an American painter turned inventor. In the 1830s, after the tragic death of his wife — a death he learned about too late because news traveled so slowly — Morse became obsessed with building a faster way to communicate. That grief drove one of the most important inventions in human history.

The First Message

On May 24, 1844, Morse sent the very first long-distance telegraph message from Washington D.C. to Baltimore, Maryland. The message read: "What hath God wrought" — a quote from the Book of Numbers. The distance was about 40 miles, and it arrived instantly. The world would never be the same.

How It Spread

Telegraph lines rapidly spread across America and Europe throughout the 1840s and 1850s. By 1866, a transatlantic cable connected the United States and Britain, allowing messages to cross the ocean in minutes rather than weeks. Morse code became the universal language of communication for over a century.

SOS — The Most Famous Signal

In 1906, the international distress signal SOS was adopted — three dots, three dashes, three dots (··· — — — ···). It was famously used by the Titanic in 1912.

Morse Code Today

While telegraph lines are long gone, morse code never fully disappeared. Amateur (ham) radio operators still use it worldwide. It's used in aviation as a backup communication method. It's also used as an accessibility tool — people with limited mobility can communicate using just two inputs: short and long.

How to Read It

A dot (·) is a short signal. A dash (—) is a long one — about 3× as long. Letters are separated by a short pause, and words by a longer one. That's it!
